## STORY ARC

Everyone talks about solving the affordable housing crisis. But when someone actually
tries to build — even a single house — they hit a wall. Not a money wall. A knowledge
wall. To evaluate a piece of land you need a geotechnical engineer, a civil
engineer, a hydrologist, an environmental consultant, an architect, a surveyor, a
floodplain manager, a cost estimator — and someone who understands building code,
zoning law, environmental permits, and how all of these disciplines interact.

That's not one expert. That's an entire multi-domain team — just to answer: "Can I
build here?"

Scale that to a developer evaluating lots, or a city screening sites for affordable
housing, and the problem multiplies.

We're SMC Labs — civil and structural engineering PhD graduates at Arizona State
University — and we're building SiteSense: an AI-native system of synthetic domain
experts that orchestrates land feasibility, construction assessment, environmental
screening, building code analysis, cost estimation, and site design — the same work
that normally requires hiring an architect, a geotechnical engineer, a structural
engineer, a hydrologist, a surveyor, an environmental consultant, a floodplain manager,
a cost estimator, and a permit specialist.

## MUSIC & TONE
- **Style:** High-energy opening (team intro), then cinematic and authoritative for
  the technical sections, warm and hopeful for the impact close.
- **Pace:** Scene 1 is fast and energetic. Scenes 2-5 are confident and measured.
  Scenes 6-7 pick up warmth. Scene 8-9 land with conviction.
- **Suggestions:** Epidemic Sound — "innovation documentary" or "tech with heart".
  Something with a build — starts light, crescendos at the expert panel, resolves warm.

---

## EDITING NOTES
- Scene 1 (crisis) should PUNCH — stats hit hard, then the human cost lands quietly. The
  mother at the table, the family moving, the rent signs — these should breathe, not rush.
  Sound design matters here: low rumble under the stats, quiet ambient under the human shots,
  then a subtle build as the empty lots and split screen reveal the gap.
- Scene 2 (team + problem) energy shifts — SMC Labs intro is fast and confident, then slows
  to let the audience feel the weight of nine specialists needed
- Scene 4 (chatbot vs brain) split-screen is the most important visual — make it sharp and punchy
- Scene 5 (expert panel) animation should feel like a coordinated team activating
- Scene 6 (live demo) is all screen recording — clean, zoomed, no distractions
- Keep text overlays clean — white on dark, sans-serif font (Inter or similar)
- Use the presentation slides as reference for diagram styling
- The "We're SMC Labs" energy should bookend the video — open and close with team identity

[SCENE 1 — stats hit hard, show the human cost, then the problem]

The U.S. affordable housing crisis affects over 75% of Americans and costs
the economy roughly 2 trillion dollars annually in lost productivity. In 2024,
nearly half of all renters were cost-burdened — spending more than 30% of
their income just on rent.

The land exists. The need is urgent. But building on it?

Everyone wants to solve this. But here's what nobody talks about.

Say a family wants to build a home. Before they pour a single foundation,
someone has to answer: Is this soil stable or will it shift? Is this in
a flood zone? What does the building code require? What are the environmental
permits? What will the site work cost? What can actually fit on this lot?

That's not one question. That's an entire team of specialists. A geotechnical
engineer. A civil engineer. A hydrologist. An environmental consultant.
An architect. A licensed surveyor. A floodplain manager. A cost estimator.
A zoning and permit specialist.

Now imagine a developer evaluating ten lots. Or a city trying to screen
fifty sites for affordable housing. You need all of those experts — for
every single parcel.

[SCENE 2 — energy shift, team + product reveal]

We're SMC Labs — a team of civil and structural engineering PhD graduates
at Arizona State University. We work at the intersection of AI and domain
expert knowledge.

And that's why we built SiteSense.

Draw a boundary on any parcel in the United States. In under 30 seconds,
SiteSense gives you a full feasibility assessment — the same work that
would normally require hiring nine different specialists.

[SCENE 3 — what it does, momentum building]

SiteSense pulls 14 verified government data layers in parallel — elevation
from USGS, flood zones from FEMA, soil properties from USDA, seismic hazard,
wetlands, fire risk, contamination from the EPA, precipitation from NOAA —
all free, all real, all sourced.

Then synthetic domain experts — each operating under real engineering code
and building law — analyze that evidence: foundation engineering, stormwater
compliance, site design, environmental permitting, cost estimation, and
architectural feasibility. An orchestrating strategist merges their findings.
And a data quality auditor governs the whole system — blocking overconfident
claims when evidence is weak.

[SCENE 4 — authoritative, technical credibility]

And this is not a chatbot.

A chatbot guesses the soil type. SiteSense queries USDA and gets the actual
USCS classification, Atterberg limits, and bearing capacity.

A chatbot does its own math — and gets it wrong. SiteSense runs deterministic
calculations first: slope, cut-and-fill, foundation type from the IBC code
ladder, stormwater runoff, cost estimation. The AI never touches the math.

A chatbot gives you one blob of text. SiteSense gives you structured, sourced,
auditable findings — every claim tagged to its origin.

[SCENE 5 — expert panel, build momentum]

Here's what's under the hood.

The Foundation Advisor applies IBC Table 1806.2 and ACI 360R-10 to recommend
a foundation type from soil, slope, and flood data. The Stormwater Reviewer
evaluates drainage using FEMA flood zones and soil hydrologic group. The Site
Design Advisor scores nine pad positions and eight orientations based on
terrain and climate. The Environmental Specialist screens for wetland permits
under Clean Water Act Section 404, endangered species under ESA Section 7,
and historic preservation under NHPA Section 106.

The Cost Forecaster projects costs with regional multipliers and 10-year
inflation. The Data Quality Auditor can downgrade any verdict if evidence
is weak.

And the Parcel Strategist ties it all together — detecting compound risks
that only appear when multiple signals combine. Like expansive soil on a
slope creating differential settlement. Or flood zone plus poor-draining
soil requiring both elevated construction and a detention basin.

Fourteen compound risk checks. Found by code. Extended by AI.

Site design recommendations with optimal building pad and orientation.
